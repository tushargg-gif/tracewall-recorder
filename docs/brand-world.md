# Tracewall — The Keep (brand world)

**Date:** 2026-06-20 · **What this is:** the creative direction that turns Tracewall from another security SaaS into a brand with a world — *the wall, and the knight who mans it.* Everything here is a tool for writing copy, designing the identity, and making the product feel like it has a spine.

---

## The big idea

Most agent-security tools sound like compliance software. Tracewall is a **stronghold**. You've hired powerful, fast, slightly-untrustworthy blades — coding agents — and let them inside your walls. Tracewall is the **wall** that rings your systems, the **knight** who stands the watch at the gate, and the **chronicle** that records every deed. You sleep because the wall holds.

The name does double duty: **Trace** (the chronicle — every action seen and recorded) + **Wall** (the rampart — nothing passes unchecked). Lean into both.

**Tagline (locked):**
> ## The knight between your agents and disaster.
> *From supply-chain vulnerability to agent actions you can't trace, Tracewall keeps the fort secure.*

---

## The world, mapped to the product

Every product concept has a place in the keep. Use this map to name features, write copy, and design states.

| In the product | In the world |
|---|---|
| Tracewall | The wall, the gatehouse, the watch |
| You (the user) | The castellan — lord of the keep |
| Coding agents (Claude Code, Codex…) | Hired blades — strong and fast, not yet trusted loose inside the walls |
| An agent action | A figure approaching the gate |
| **Allow** | The gate opens — passage granted |
| **Ask / escalate** | *"Halt — who goes there?"* The watch calls for your word (a parley) |
| **Deny / block** | The portcullis drops |
| Policy | The law of the keep |
| Policy *by demonstration* | You don't write the law from a dusty tome — you pass judgment on real deeds, and the law writes itself |
| The audit log (hash-chained, tamper-evident) | **The Chronicle** — every deed recorded and **sealed**; the seal can't be forged |
| Supply-chain attack | A Trojan horse at the gate / a poisoned shipment |
| A rogue agent action | A blade turned |
| Trust earned over time | Knights who've proven loyal ride through unchallenged |
| Compliance / evidence export | Present the Chronicle to the king's auditors |
| The repo / your systems | The keep itself — what's worth defending |

This is why **courage** belongs in the theme: the wall lets you act *boldly*. You let your agents move fast because something has your back. *"Move boldly. The wall holds."*

## Voice & lexicon

Confident, courageous, a touch mythic — but modern and clear. **We earn gravitas; we don't cosplay.** The reader is a serious engineer or security lead, not a LARPer.

**Use:** wall · watch · gate · the line · the Chronicle · the keep · sealed · vigilance · courage · "nothing passes unseen" · "hold the line" · "stand your watch" · "every deed, recorded."

**Avoid:** olde-English (no *thee/thou/'tis*), wizards/dragons/fantasy, anything that reads like a video game, and (still) hype and exclamation marks. The medievalism lives in *nouns and posture*, not in costume.

**Sample lines**
- "Nothing passes unseen."
- "Move boldly. The wall holds."
- "Every deed, recorded in the Chronicle."
- "Your agents move fast. We keep the gate."
- "Hold the line against what you can't watch yourself."

## Visual direction

The trick: a **brand layer** carries the full fort/knight world; the **product UI** stays clean and functional. Flavor, not costume.

**Palette — two layers.**
- *Functional (inside the app — unchanged):* the verdict system stays — green = passage granted (allow), amber = halt/parley (ask), red = portcullis (deny), violet = the AI's counsel (policy suggestions). This is load-bearing UX; don't touch it.
- *Heraldic (brand / marketing / identity — new):* iron & stone neutrals (blackened charcoal, slate, cold stone-grey) as the canvas; **old-gold / brass** as the heraldic accent (the crest, the seal, courage); deep **midnight navy** for depth and *mystery*; deep crimson reserved for banners/alerts (rhymes with red=deny). The marketing site can be **dark and regal** even though the app is clean and light.

**Mark — a crest.** A shield bearing a **battlement/wall** motif and a **portcullis** (the portcullis grid also reads as a *trace* / audit lattice — perfect double meaning). A **wax-seal** motif for the tamper-evident Chronicle. Wordmark "Tracewall" in a strong, lightly-**engraved** serif or a confident geometric with a heraldic touch — **never blackletter** (illegible, kitsch).

**Motifs & textures (use sparingly = premium):** battlement silhouette, portcullis grid, wax seal, banner/pennant, restrained gold foil, etched/engraved linework, cold stone texture.

**Type:** a display serif with gravitas for headlines + Inter for UI/body + JetBrains Mono for commands and the Chronicle. (Exact fonts chosen in the visual pass.)

---

## The one guardrail — premium, not kitsch

The failure mode of a knight theme is looking like a *game*. We avoid it with **heraldic minimalism**: think a special-forces insignia, a central bank's coat of arms, or a great university's crest — institutions that use medieval cues to signal *trust and permanence*, rendered in a modern, restrained, expensive way. One crest, one gold, a lot of stone and space. Every medieval cue must also make *security* sense (a portcullis = a gate; a seal = tamper-evidence; a chronicle = an audit log). If a cue is only decoration, cut it.

**Where the world shows up loud:** logo/crest, the landing-page hero, launch assets, the pitch deck, swag, the README hero.
**Where it whispers:** the product UI — the crest in the corner, "the Chronicle" as the audit-log name, a portcullis motif on the loading state, the verdict colors doing the real work.
